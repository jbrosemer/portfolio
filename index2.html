SUSTAINABLE DESIGN IN ALGIERS POINT

Vernacular Architecture and Algiers Point

Vernacular architecture uses sustainable designs that evolve over time to maximize the efficiency of buildings with respect to regional climate, culture, techniques and resources.

Established in 1719, Algiers is the second oldest neighborhood in New Orleans and has a rich history that is advantageous for our study of sustainable attributes of the built environment. Although part of the city of New Orleans, Algiers Point, influenced by French settlements and Spanish colonialism, is isolated on the West Bank and is separated from the French Quarter by the Mississippi River. It was an established settlement for freed slaves and later on a center for industry. Despite its significant history, the area is undergoing rapid transformation. It is because of this changing condition that it provides a rich topic of study. Contemporary design trends provide context for evaluating sustainable design techniques that have evolved over centuries.

House Types


One of the many things that makes New Orleans such a fascinating city for architecture is the variety of building types and styles. A range of traditions and cultures have come together since the city was first founded to shape the built environment. Over time, the needs and tastes of people residing in these homes or occupying these structures changes. Thus, these “spaces gain a social identity and personal significance” (Allen, 2017).

Many different types and styles are present in New Orleans and Algiers Point houses, specifically. House type generally encompasses the room arrangement, the scale, and the massing of a home. Because many house types have been popular for long periods of time, they are often associated with several different housing styles. House style typically refers to the outer appearance of the house and its decorative elements. This includes the materials used, the ornamentation, and the overall features of the house’s facade.

Function of Ornamentation


Various elements of ornamentation can be seen across New Orleans among all house types and styles. Although ornamentation is a way of decorating and adding character to a home, it also serves a functional purpose. Shutters, vents, brackets, galleries, and transoms all aim to provide embellishment to the building’s structure while protecting against weather or controlling for temperature. Intricately patterned ironwork is used to provide security to windows and projected firewalls prevent fires from spreading from one house to another.

Sustainable Attributes

One integral characteristic of vernacular architecture is the emphasis on sustainability. Sustainable design is achieving the design goals that allow for comfort and appealing aesthetics using methods that allow our natural resources to be used responsibly, so that current generations do not jeopardize them for future generations. Many sustainable building strategies are developed to fit the local climate or meet common challenges of a particular region.
